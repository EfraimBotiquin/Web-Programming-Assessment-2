About Venus

Venus is the second planet from the Sun and is known as the hottest planet in the solar system. Its thick atmosphere traps heat, creating a strong greenhouse effect.

The planet is covered with clouds of sulfuric acid, making it very bright in the sky. Venus rotates very slowly and in the opposite direction compared to most planets.

Venus is the second planet from the Sun and is often called Earth’s twin because it is similar in size and mass. However, its environment is extremely different from Earth in many important ways. Venus has a thick atmosphere made mostly of carbon dioxide, with clouds of sulfuric acid that trap heat and create a powerful runaway greenhouse effect. This intense heat buildup makes the surface extremely hostile and not suitable for life as we know it. This makes Venus the hottest planet in the Solar System, even hotter than Mercury, despite being farther from the Sun. The pressure on the surface is also extremely high, and conditions are harsh enough to crush most spacecraft.

The planet rotates very slowly and in the opposite direction to most planets, which is known as retrograde rotation. This means that on Venus, the Sun would appear to rise in the west and set in the east, which is the opposite of what we experience on Earth. Its rocky surface includes vast plains, mountain ranges, and volcanic features, showing that it has a complex geological history. However, the dense and cloudy atmosphere makes detailed observation of the surface very difficult, so most of what scientists know comes from radar imaging and space missions. Because of these extreme conditions, Venus is not easy to explore directly. Venus remains very important for scientists because it helps them understand how planetary atmospheres behave under extreme conditions, how climate change can develop on a large scale, and why Earth evolved into a more habitable world while Venus became so hostile and different over time.
