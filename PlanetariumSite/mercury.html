About Mercury

Mercury is the closest planet to the Sun and the smallest planet.

It has extreme temperatures and a cratered surface.

Mercury is the smallest planet in the Solar System and the one located closest to the Sun, making it the innermost planet in our planetary system. It has a heavily cratered surface, a large iron-rich core at its center, and almost no atmosphere to trap heat or protect it from space conditions. As a result, Mercury experiences extreme temperature changes, with scorching hot days and freezing cold nights across its surface. It also moves very quickly around the Sun, completing one full orbit in just 88 Earth days, which makes it the fastest planet in the entire Solar System.

Mercury's slow rotation creates a very unusual day-night cycle compared to most planets, meaning that a single day on Mercury (from one sunrise to the next) lasts much longer than a Mercury year might suggest. This happens because the planet rotates very slowly on its axis while it orbits the Sun quickly, creating a unique timing pattern for daylight and darkness. Since Mercury has no moons and almost no atmosphere to erode or protect its surface, it remains heavily covered with ancient impact craters that have stayed unchanged for billions of years. These conditions make its surface look very similar to how it was in the early history of the Solar System. Spacecraft missions such as Mariner 10 and MESSENGER have been extremely important in helping scientists study Mercury in detail. They provided valuable data about its geology, chemical composition, magnetic field, and its unusual orbital and rotational behavior.
