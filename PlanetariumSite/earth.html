About Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known planet that supports life. It has liquid water on its surface, a protective atmosphere, and a stable range of temperatures that allow ecosystems to thrive. About 71% of Earth is covered by oceans, lakes, and seas, which play a major role in regulating climate and supporting life. These bodies of water help distribute heat around the planet, influencing weather patterns and maintaining balance in the environment. Its diverse landscapes, including mountains, forests, deserts, and oceans, provide habitats for a wide variety of plants and animals. Together, these features make Earth a unique and dynamic planet within the Solar System.

Earth also has one natural satellite, the Moon, which plays an important role in affecting ocean tides and helping to stabilize the planet's rotation over long periods of time. This stability contributes to a consistent climate, which is essential for sustaining life. The gravitational interaction between Earth and the Moon also influences the length of our days and the movement of ocean waters. In addition, Earth's atmosphere, magnetic field, and active geology make it one of the most dynamic and constantly changing planets in the Solar System. Volcanic activity, earthquakes, and the movement of tectonic plates continuously reshape the surface. Scientists continue studying Earth because understanding our home planet helps improve climate science, weather forecasting, and environmental protection. This knowledge is essential for managing natural resources and preparing for future environmental challenges.
